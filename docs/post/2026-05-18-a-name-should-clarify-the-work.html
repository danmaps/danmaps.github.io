A Name Should Clarify the Work

This week did not produce a flashy new app.

It produced something quieter and probably more important: a better way to describe the work I actually want to do.

I spent some time pressure-testing McVey Data Ops as a possible umbrella name.

That might sound like branding theater, but I do not think it is.

Names are not just decoration. They are compression. A good one helps you explain what kind of problems you solve before you say anything else. A bad one creates cleanup work.

The real question was not "is this catchy?"

The question was simpler:

Does this name make the work legible?

That matters because the work is not only one thing anymore.

It includes:

  • spatial analysis and GIS workflows
  • automation
  • internal tools
  • data-heavy operational systems
  • AI-assisted tooling where the AI is useful, but not the entire story

A more novelty-driven AI name could overstate the wrong thing.
A more GIS-specific name could box the work in too early.
A more playful personal brand might be fun, but less credible in the environments I may actually be selling into.

Why "McVey Data Ops" seems to work

The strongest thing about it is that it sounds like a place where practical work happens.

Not inspiration.
Not thought leadership.
Not "labs" in the vague startup sense.

Operational work.

That fits the direction better than a lot of alternatives because the thread running through the projects is not "I love data" or "I love maps" in the abstract.
It is closer to this:

  • take messy real-world information
  • turn it into a workflow
  • make the workflow lighter, clearer, or more useful
  • build the smallest viable tool that helps someone act on it

That is what the name is doing well.

It leaves room for:

  • GIS automation
  • dashboards
  • lightweight apps
  • agent-assisted workflows
  • spatial decision support
  • internal operational tooling

without forcing all of it through one overly narrow label.

The useful test: can it survive contact with reality?

What helped this week was not endless naming ideation. It was testing the name against actual surfaces.

Things like:

  • a homepage header
  • a LinkedIn headline
  • a proposal intro
  • an open-source repo footer

That is a much better test than asking whether a name sounds cool in isolation.

A name can feel clever in a note and still become awkward the second it has to do real work.

The draft positioning that made this click for me was something like:

Operational tools for spatial data, automation, and AI workflows.

That is not trying to win a branding award.
It is trying to tell the truth in one line.

I increasingly think that is the right standard.

This is really a scope decision disguised as branding

The more I thought about it, the more obvious it became that this is not only about naming.

It is about scope.

A name is a bet on what category of work you plan to keep doing.

If I chose something tightly locked to GIS, I would be saying:

  • maps first
  • everything else as a sidecar

If I chose something centered entirely on AI, I would be saying:

  • model-centric work first
  • the operational and spatial parts as supporting material

Neither one feels quite right.

The work I seem to keep coming back to lives in the overlap:

  • systems
  • operations
  • data
  • spatial context when it matters
  • automation when it helps
  • AI where it earns its keep

That is why the "Ops" part matters.
It points toward implementation and usefulness, not just analysis or aesthetics.

Boring is underrated when it is accurate

I do not think this is the most dazzling name in the world.

That may be fine.

There is a kind of boring that is actually a strength.
The boring names that age well usually do one of two things:

  • they describe the work clearly
  • they create enough room for the work to evolve

If a name is memorable but misleading, the memorability is not helping.
If it is slightly plain but sturdy, that can be an asset.

Especially for work that is likely to live in enterprise, utility, public-sector, or operations-heavy environments, credibility is a feature.

My takeaway

The small lesson from this week is that naming is worth taking seriously when it clarifies strategy.

Not because branding is magic.
Because a good name can reduce friction between:

  • what you have done
  • what you want to do more of
  • and how other people understand the gap between those two things

Right now, McVey Data Ops feels like a plausible umbrella for that.

Not final destiny. Just a better fit than forcing the work into the wrong story.

And that is enough progress to count.
