AI Needs Real Work

Lately I have been trying to apply a stricter test to AI.

Not whether it is impressive.
Not whether it can generate a lot.
Not whether it makes me feel productive.

The real question is simpler: does it help me do real work?

By real work, I mean things like clarifying a decision, turning a vague idea into a plan, creating a usable artifact, reducing mental overhead, or helping move a project from scattered thoughts into an actual system.

That sounds obvious, but I think it matters because the current AI discourse still has a weird tendency to reward spectacle over usefulness.

I do not need more spectacle.
I need less friction.

The test I keep coming back to

Over the last week, a few threads kept converging for me.

One was personal and practical: trying to get clearer about what value I am actually getting from AI tools.
Another was product-oriented: tightening how I think about positioning and usefulness for geospatial tools instead of drifting into broad, fuzzy ambition.
A third was more general: noticing that AI is most valuable when it helps collapse ambiguity.

That has become my working standard:

AI is useful when it helps turn vague thinking into clear next steps, artifacts, and decisions.

If it does not do that, then a lot of what it produces is just activity with better branding.

Where it has been genuinely useful

The strongest uses are not the flashy ones.

They are things like:

  • turning rough ideas into structured notes or briefs
  • surfacing tradeoffs I had not made explicit yet
  • helping me evaluate whether a project has a real wedge or just an interesting shape
  • reducing the amount of context I have to keep in my head at once
  • producing drafts that are good enough to react to, revise, or discard

That last part matters.
A draft is valuable if it moves the work forward, even if the draft is not the final answer.

I think that is the right frame for a lot of AI usage right now. Not replacement. Not magic. Not autonomous genius. Just a cheap, flexible planning-and-execution layer that helps convert mental clutter into something inspectable.

What I am less interested in

I am increasingly suspicious of AI workflows that generate a lot without making judgment any clearer.

A giant pile of code is not necessarily progress.
A polished block of prose is not necessarily insight.
A long answer is not necessarily decision support.

If anything, the danger is that AI makes low-value output feel more substantial than it is.

That is why I keep coming back to inspectability.
I want systems where the result can be examined, challenged, edited, and turned into the next concrete action.

That applies to writing.
It applies to product thinking.
It applies to geospatial work.
And it definitely applies to software.

The standard I want to keep

My current test is boring on purpose.

At the end of the month, I should be able to point to something and say:

  • this decision became clearer
  • this plan got structured
  • this project moved forward
  • this artifact exists now
  • this thing no longer had to live entirely in my head

If I cannot say that, then I was probably consuming AI rather than using it.

That feels like the distinction that matters.

Not whether AI is amazing.
Whether it is useful enough to justify the space it takes up in my tools, attention, and life.

Right now, I think the best case for it is not future hype.
It is present-tense utility.

That is a much better standard.
